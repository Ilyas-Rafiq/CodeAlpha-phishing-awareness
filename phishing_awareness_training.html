How to spot a phishing email

Red flags to watch for
Urgent or threatening language ("act now!")
Sender address doesn't match display name
Suspicious or mismatched links
Unexpected attachments
Generic greetings ("Dear Customer")
Poor grammar or unusual formatting
Requests for passwords or financial info
Too-good-to-be-true offers
Spotting fake websites
Check the URL carefully — phishers use "paypa1.com" or "amazon-secure.net" instead of the real domain
Look for HTTPS, but remember: HTTPS alone doesn't mean a site is legitimate
Watch for subtle typos: "arnazon.com", "micros0ft.com", "g00gle.com"
Hover over links before clicking to see the real destination URL
Fake login pages often look pixel-perfect — always type URLs directly instead of clicking links

Social engineering tactics attackers use

Urgency & Fear
Attackers create a false sense of emergency to prevent you from thinking critically. When people feel panicked, they skip verification steps.
"Your account has been compromised! Click here within 24 hours or your account will be permanently deleted."
Legitimate services almost never demand immediate action or threaten account deletion via email.
Authority Impersonation
Attackers pose as CEOs, IT admins, banks, government agencies, or tech companies. People are psychologically conditioned to comply with authority figures.
"This is the IT Security team. We need your credentials immediately to secure your account before a breach spreads."
Real IT teams never ask for your password — not by email, phone, or in person.
Familiarity & Trust
Spear phishing uses personal details harvested from LinkedIn, social media, or data breaches to craft highly personalized messages that feel genuine.
"Hi Sarah, it's John from accounting. I saw you worked on the Q3 report — can you check this invoice and approve urgently?"
Always verify unusual financial requests through a second channel (a phone call, not a reply email).
Curiosity & Reward
Promises of prizes, discounts, or exclusive content lure victims into clicking. This exploits natural human curiosity and desire for reward.
"You've been selected for our exclusive loyalty reward! Claim your $500 Amazon gift card — offer expires today."
If an offer seems too good to be true, it almost certainly is. Go directly to the company's official website.
Smishing & Vishing
Phishing isn't only email. SMS phishing (smishing) and voice phishing (vishing) use text messages and phone calls to trick victims — and can be even more convincing.
SMS: "USPS: Your package is held. Verify your address: usps-delivery[.]net/track" — Phone: "This is Microsoft Support, your computer is sending error reports."
Never call back numbers from suspicious SMS. Hang up and call the official number from the organization's website.

Annotated real-world phishing email

From:security@paypal-account-alert.com
Real PayPal address is @paypal.com. "paypal-account-alert.com" is a fake domain registered by attackers.
 Fake domain
To:you@yourcompany.com
Subject:⚠️ Your PayPal account has been LIMITED — Verify Now
Urgency + emoji in subject line is a manipulation tactic. Real PayPal subjects are plain and professional.

Dear Customer,


We have detected unusual activity on your PayPal account. Your account access has been temporarily limited.


You must verify your information within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended.


👉 Verify My Account Now →


Thank you,
The PayPal Security Team

Fake sender domain: "paypal-account-alert.com" is not PayPal. Always check the full email address, not just the display name.
Artificial urgency: "24 hours or permanently suspended" — designed to panic you into acting without thinking.
Generic greeting: "Dear Customer" — PayPal always uses your registered full name.
What to do: Don't click the link. Open a new browser tab and type paypal.com directly. If there's really an issue, you'll see it there.
Other common phishing scenarios
Package delivery scam: Fake FedEx/UPS SMS asking you to "reschedule delivery" via a link that steals your credit card.
CEO fraud (BEC): Attacker emails finance team posing as the CEO demanding an urgent wire transfer to a new vendor.
Cloud service phishing: Fake Microsoft 365 / Google login page that captures your credentials for corporate account takeover.

Best practices to stay safe

Before you click
Hover over any link to preview the real destination URL before clicking
Verify the sender's email address — not just the display name
When in doubt, go directly to the website by typing it in your browser
Call the sender through official contact info to confirm unusual requests
Technical defenses
Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts — even if your password is stolen, attackers can't log in
Use a password manager — it won't autofill credentials on fake lookalike sites
Keep software and browsers updated — patches fix security vulnerabilities exploited by malicious links
Use a reputable email spam filter and don't disable it
If you think you've been phished
Don't panic — report it to your IT/security team immediately
Change your passwords on affected accounts right away
Contact your bank if financial details were shared
Forward phishing emails to reportphishing@apwg.org or your organization's security team
